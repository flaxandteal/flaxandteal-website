Safety first — read before you start

All the electronics in this project run on 5 volts DC — the same voltage as a phone charger. This is safe for humans. However, incorrect wiring can damage the Raspberry Pi or start a small electrical fire. Follow these rules every time.

🚫

Most important rule: power off when wiring

Never connect or disconnect wires while the power supply is plugged in. Always unplug the power before making any changes to the wiring. Plug power in only at the specific test points in this guide.

1

Power off before wiring

Unplug the 5V power supply before connecting any wires. Only power on when this guide tells you to.

2

Touch metal before handling the Pi

Static electricity from your hands can damage the Raspberry Pi. Touch a metal radiator or tap before picking it up. This is called "earthing yourself".

3

Mount heaters on non-flammable surfaces

The PTC heating elements reach 40°C — warm, not burning-hot. But place them on an aluminium plate or ceramic tile, never directly on cardboard, foam, or raw wood inside the model.

4

Double-check wires before powering on

Use the pre-power checklist in Chapter 6 before connecting your first power supply. A quick check takes 2 minutes and prevents most problems.

5

Do not short circuit the power rail

Never connect a red (+5V) wire directly to a black (GND) wire without something in between. This creates a short circuit and can cause heat or smoke.

6

No mains voltage in this project

Everything here is low-voltage DC (5V). Never connect mains electricity (the wall socket) to any component in this project.

Getting to know your parts

A friendly tour of every component before you start

Before connecting anything, take a few minutes to understand what each component does. This makes the wiring steps much less confusing.

The brain

Raspberry Pi 4B

A tiny computer that runs Python code. It has 40 metal pins (the "GPIO header") along one edge — these are how it talks to other electronics.

💡 Think of it as a very small PC that can directly control physical things.
Control board

Automation HAT Mini

Clips onto the Pi's GPIO pins. Provides a relay switch and several inputs/outputs. It also has a small display screen. Has one relay (not three — the full HAT has three).

💡 Like an expansion pack for the Pi — adds real-world switching capability.
Temperature sensors

DS18B20 Waterproof Probe

Three-wire sensor in a metal probe. Measures temperature digitally. All three sensors can share a single wire back to the Pi — each has a unique ID so the Pi knows which is which.

💡 Like a digital thermometer that sends readings directly to the Pi.
Heating element

PTC Heater (FIT0844)

A flat, 5V ceramic heating element. "PTC" stands for Positive Temperature Coefficient — the material physically self-limits at 40°C. It cannot overheat by design. No thermostat needed.

💡 Like a tiny, smart heated blanket that switches itself off at the right temperature.
Switching component

IRLZ44N MOSFET

An electronic switch you can trigger with 3.3V from the Pi. One tiny signal on the "gate" pin allows or blocks a much larger current flowing through it. Runs completely silently — no moving parts.

💡 Like a tap: a small turn of the handle (the Pi's signal) controls a large flow (the heater's power).
Mechanical switch

Relay (inside the HAT)

An electrically-operated mechanical switch. When the Pi sends a signal, a small magnet pulls a metal contact closed, completing a separate circuit. Has three terminals: COM (common), NO (normally open), and NC (normally closed).

💡 Like a light switch, but flipped by electricity instead of your finger.
Power connection

Stacking Header

A special 40-pin connector that goes between the Pi and the HAT Mini. The HAT sits on top, but the long pins of the stacking header stick out above and below so you can connect wires to any pin.

💡 Like an extension lead — plugs in between two things to give you extra access points.
Wire junction

WAGO Lever-Nut

A small transparent connector with a lever. Push a wire in, close the lever, and it's held firmly. Lift the lever to release. No soldering needed. Great for joining multiple wires in the model house.

💡 Like a tiny, reusable junction box for wires.

Preparing your Raspberry Pi

Install the OS, configure settings, and enable the interfaces you need

ℹ️

Before you start this chapter

You will need a laptop or desktop with internet access to prepare the microSD card. The official Raspberry Pi Imager is free and handles everything.

Download and install the Raspberry Pi Imager

On your laptop, go to raspberrypi.com/software and download the Raspberry Pi Imager for your operating system. Install and open it.

Flash Raspberry Pi OS onto your microSD card

Insert the microSD card into your laptop using an adaptor if needed. In the Imager:

  • Choose Device: Raspberry Pi 4
  • Choose OS: Raspberry Pi OS (64-bit) — the one labelled "Recommended"
  • Choose Storage: select your microSD card

Click Next, then click Edit Settings. This is important — you can set up Wi-Fi, a username, password, and enable SSH here before the card even boots. Fill in your Wi-Fi details and a hostname (e.g. model-house). Enable SSH under the Services tab. Click Save, then Yes when prompted.

Click Write. This takes 3–5 minutes.

First boot

Insert the microSD card into the slot on the underside of the Pi (it clicks in). Connect a monitor via the micro-HDMI port (use the port labelled HDMI0 — closest to the USB-C power port), keyboard, and mouse if using a screen directly. Alternatively, connect the Pi to power and SSH in from your laptop over Wi-Fi using the hostname you set.

To SSH from your laptop, open a Terminal (Mac/Linux) or PowerShell (Windows) and type:

In your laptop's terminal
$ ssh pi@model-house.local

Replace pi with your username and model-house with the hostname you set. Accept the fingerprint prompt by typing yes.

Enable I2C and 1-Wire interfaces

The HAT Mini uses I2C to communicate with the Pi. The temperature sensors use 1-Wire. Both must be enabled. Once connected to the Pi (via SSH or directly), run:

On the Pi
$ sudo raspi-config

Navigate to Interface Options. Enable I2C. Then go back to Interface Options and enable 1-Wire. Select Finish and reboot when prompted.

After reboot, open the config file to confirm the 1-Wire overlay is pointing to the correct pin:

On the Pi — check the config file
$ grep w1 /boot/firmware/config.txt

You should see a line containing dtoverlay=w1-gpio. If it does not already say gpiopin=4, open the file to edit it:

Edit the config file
$ sudo nano /boot/firmware/config.txt

Find the line and change it to read exactly:

dtoverlay=w1-gpio,gpiopin=4

Press Ctrl+X, then Y, then Enter to save. Reboot: sudo reboot.

Fitting the Automation HAT Mini

Solder the stacking header, attach the HAT, and understand the relay terminals

⚡

Power off for this entire chapter

The Pi must be completely unpowered. No USB-C cable, no power supply connected. Confirm the Pi is off before touching any pins.

🔥

First soldering in this project

This chapter requires soldering the stacking header onto the HAT Mini. If you have never soldered before, search for "how to solder a through-hole header" on YouTube — a 5-minute video will teach you everything you need. Work slowly, one pin at a time.

Prepare the stacking header

The Automation HAT Mini comes with a standard female header. You need to replace this with the extra-tall stacking header so you can access the GPIO pins from above the HAT.

If the HAT Mini already has a header soldered on, you will need to carefully desolder it first. If it is just inserted (not soldered) or you are building from scratch, insert the stacking header into the HAT Mini's GPIO holes from the underside of the board — the long pins should point downward (toward the Pi), and the female sockets should point upward (above the HAT).

Solder each of the 40 pins on the top side of the HAT Mini board. Use just enough solder to fill the hole and form a small dome — avoid blobs that could bridge two adjacent pins. Allow to cool fully before proceeding.

Seat the HAT Mini onto the Pi

With the Pi upside-down so you can see the GPIO pins, carefully align the 40 long pins of the stacking header with the Pi's GPIO header. All 40 pins must align — take your time and do not force it. Press down gently but firmly until the HAT Mini sits flush against the Pi's PCB.

The HAT Mini's screw-terminal strip (the row of small green connectors) should be at the same end as the Pi's USB ports.

💡

Check alignment before pressing down

Hold both boards up to the light. Every Pi GPIO pin should enter a stacking header socket — none should be between sockets or off to the side. A slightly misaligned pin can bend and break.

Understand the HAT Mini's relay terminals

Look at the HAT Mini — along one edge there is a row of screw terminals. The relay terminals are labelled on the PCB silkscreen. Identify the three relay terminals:

  • NO — Normally Open. The circuit is open (off) when the relay is not activated.
  • COM — Common. Always connected internally to either NO or NC.
  • NC — Normally Closed. The circuit is closed (on) when the relay is not activated.

For Heater 1, you will use NO and COM — so the heater is OFF by default and switches ON when the Pi activates the relay. Never wire anything to NC in this project.

ℹ️

The HAT Mini has one relay — not three

The full-size Automation HAT has three relays. The Mini has only one. This guide uses one relay (for Heater 1) plus two separate MOSFET circuits (for Heater 2 and the fan) — giving you control of all three actuators.

Connecting the temperature sensors

Wire all three DS18B20 sensors onto a single data bus

ℹ️

How three sensors share one wire

The DS18B20 uses a protocol called "1-Wire". Every sensor has a unique ID burned into it at the factory. All three sensors connect to the same three wires (power, ground, and data) — the Pi reads all three simultaneously and tells them apart by their unique IDs. This means you only need one connection to the Pi, no matter how many sensors you add.

Identify the sensor wires

Each DS18B20 probe has three wires coming out of the cable. The standard colour code for the PiHut DS18B20 is:

🔴 Red = Power (VCC) — connect to 3.3V
⚫ Black = Ground (GND) — connect to GND
🟡 Yellow = Data — connect to GPIO 4
⚠️

Always verify wire colours with a multimeter

Wire colours vary between manufacturers and even between batches. Before connecting anything, use your multimeter in continuity mode (the "beep" mode) to confirm which wire connects to which pin on the sensor head. Check the sensor's own datasheet or marking.

Build the sensor bus on your perfboard

All three sensors share one data bus. Rather than connecting directly to the Pi, you will build a small junction on the perfboard where the three sensor cables meet, and then run a single set of three wires from there to the Pi.

On your perfboard, solder three rows of 4 connected holes (or use WAGO lever-nuts):

  • A red/VCC row: joins all three red wires + one wire to the Pi's 3.3V pin
  • A black/GND row: joins all three black wires + one wire to the Pi's GND pin
  • A yellow/DATA row: joins all three yellow wires + one wire to the Pi's GPIO 4 pin

The 4.7kΩ pull-up resistor is soldered between the VCC row and the DATA row. One end goes to VCC (3.3V), the other end goes to DATA. You need only one resistor regardless of how many sensors are on the bus.

Connect the sensor bus to the Pi (via stacking header)

Run three Dupont wires from your perfboard junction up to the stacking header on the HAT Mini stack. These are the only five connections you need from the stacking header for the sensors:

  • Pin 1 (3.3V) → to the red VCC bus on your perfboard
  • Pin 7 (GPIO 4) → to the yellow DATA bus on your perfboard
  • Pin 9 (GND) → to the black GND bus on your perfboard

Position the sensors in the model house

Place the three sensors at these locations before securing the cables:

  • Sensor 1 — one end of the model house
  • Sensor 2 — the middle of the model house
  • Sensor 3 — the other end of the model house

Route the cables neatly along the walls or floor using cable ties and adhesive mounts. Keep all sensor cables away from the heater elements.

4

Wiring the heaters

Heater 1 via the relay, Heater 2 via a MOSFET circuit

🌡️

PTC heater safety — these are safe by design

The DFRobot FIT0844 uses PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient) technology. As the element heats up, its electrical resistance increases automatically — reducing current and limiting the temperature to about 40°C. It is self-regulating and cannot overheat. The surface feels warm to the touch, like the underside of a laptop. It is not a fire hazard when mounted correctly.

Mount and protect the heaters

For each heater element:

  • Stick the heater flat onto the aluminium plate using Kapton tape — aluminium side down, heater face up.
  • Loosely wrap the assembly in a piece of cut silicone mat. The silicone lets heat escape gently while protecting surrounding materials.
  • Secure with a small loop of Kapton tape. Do not over-wrap — you want heat to dissipate into the model space, not be trapped.
  • Mount inside the model in the bed area. Place on a non-flammable surface — not directly on wood or unfinished cardboard. A thin ceramic tile offcut works perfectly.

Wire Heater 1 to the relay (NO and COM terminals)

The relay on the HAT Mini will switch Heater 1. When the Pi activates the relay, power flows through the circuit and the heater turns on.

The relay terminals are on the HAT Mini's screw terminal strip. Use a small flat-head screwdriver to loosen each screw terminal, insert the wire, and retighten firmly.

  • Connect a wire from your 5V power distribution (+) to the relay's NO terminal
  • Connect a wire from the relay's COM terminal to one wire of Heater 1
  • Connect the other wire of Heater 1 to your GND distribution (−)

Build the MOSFET circuit for Heater 2

Heater 2 is switched by an IRLZ44N MOSFET mounted on your perfboard. A MOSFET is a type of transistor — a semiconductor component that acts as an electronic switch. The Pi sends a small signal to the "Gate" pin (like pressing a switch button), and the MOSFET allows or blocks the heater's power supply through the "Drain" and "Source" pins.

The IRLZ44N has three legs (it looks like a small plastic tab with three metal prongs). Facing the flat side of the component, the pins from left to right are: Gate, Drain, Source.

Hand-drawn
MOSFET switch circuit — Heater 2 (and Fan, same layout)
MOSFET circuit — hand-drawn view

Solder the MOSFET and resistors onto your perfboard as follows:

  • Insert the IRLZ44N into the perfboard, flat face pointing away from you. Note which leg is Gate, Drain, Source (left to right when facing the flat side).
  • Solder a 220Ω resistor in series between a wire from GPIO 17 and the Gate pin.
  • Solder a 10kΩ resistor between the Gate pin and the GND rail on your perfboard. This is the pull-down — it keeps the MOSFET safely OFF when the Pi starts up.
  • Connect a wire from the Drain pin to one lead of Heater 2.
  • Connect the other lead of Heater 2 to +5V.
  • Connect the Source pin to the GND rail.
5

Wiring the AC unit fan

Same MOSFET circuit as Heater 2, with one extra component

🌀

The fan needs one extra component

Fans contain a small motor with a copper coil. When a motor is switched off quickly, the collapsing magnetic field generates a brief voltage spike in reverse direction — called back-EMF. This can damage the MOSFET. A single 1N5819 Schottky diode, wired across the fan, absorbs this spike harmlessly. The heaters do not need this because they are purely resistive (no coil).

Identify the fan wires

Your model AC unit fan has two wires. Confirm which is positive (+) and which is negative (−) by checking its label or datasheet. For a standard 5V DC fan: red = positive (+5V), black = negative (GND). If the fan uses white and black, white is typically positive — but verify before wiring.

Build the fan MOSFET circuit

The circuit is identical to Heater 2, using GPIO 27 (physical pin 13) as the control pin. Build a second IRLZ44N circuit on your perfboard with the 220Ω gate resistor and 10kΩ pull-down, as described in Chapter 4 Step 3.

The one difference: add the 1N5819 flyback diode across the fan motor terminals. The diode has a stripe on one end — that is the cathode (negative side). Wire it with the stripe toward +5V and the other end toward the Drain side. It sits in parallel with the fan.

  • Fan positive (+) → connect to +5V
  • Fan negative (−) → connect to MOSFET Drain
  • MOSFET Source → GND
  • 1N5819 diode: cathode (striped end) to +5V, anode to the fan negative / MOSFET Drain junction
  • MOSFET Gate: 220Ω from GPIO 27 (pin 13), 10kΩ pull-down to GND

Connect the control wire from the stacking header

Run a Dupont wire from physical pin 13 (GPIO 27) on the stacking header down to the fan MOSFET gate resistor input on your perfboard.

Setting up the power supply

Connect the 5V 6A supply, build the distribution block, and run the pre-power checklist

🚫

Do not connect the power supply to the mains yet

Complete all wiring connections in this chapter before plugging the supply into the wall socket. The pre-power checklist at the end of this chapter is mandatory before first power-on.

Understand the power budget

All components in this project run from 5V DC. Here is what everything draws:

ComponentSteady currentPeak / start-up
Raspberry Pi 4B + HAT Mini~1.0A~1.5A
Heater 1 (PTC)~0.4A~0.6–1.0A (cold start)
Heater 2 (PTC)~0.4A~0.6–1.0A (cold start)
AC unit fan~0.25A~0.5A (stall)
3× DS18B20 sensors~0.005ANegligible
Total~2.1A~4.5A worst-case

The 5V 6A supply provides 6 amps — about 33% headroom above worst-case peak. This is why a 3A supply (e.g. the official Pi PSU alone) would not be sufficient for the whole system.

Build the power distribution block

The power supply has screw terminal outputs labelled +V and −V (or +5V and GND). Connect these to your perfboard's 5V bus and GND bus. From there, run individual wires to each load:

  • +5V bus → Relay NO terminal, Heater 2 positive, Fan positive, and the Pi's 5V pin (GPIO pin 2 or 4) via a Dupont wire from the stacking header
  • GND bus → Heater 1 negative (from relay COM), MOSFET sources for Heater 2 and Fan, Pi GND (GPIO pin 6 or 9)
⚠️

Pi power via GPIO pins bypasses the fuse

Powering the Pi through its 5V GPIO pins (pins 2 or 4) rather than the USB-C port bypasses the Pi's built-in protection. For a fixed, enclosed project like this it is acceptable — but it means any wiring error could damage the Pi directly. Double-check polarity (red to 5V, black to GND) before proceeding. An alternative is to use a separate official Pi USB-C PSU connected to the USB-C port for the Pi, and use the main 6A supply only for the heaters and fan.

Connect the power supply to the mains — preparation

The 5V 6A enclosed PSU connects to a standard mains socket via its input terminals (L = Live, N = Neutral, PE = Earth). If you are not comfortable with mains wiring, have a qualified electrician connect the input side. The output side (5V DC) is what you have been wiring throughout this guide and is completely safe.

Many enclosed PSUs come with a mains lead already attached. If yours does not, use a standard IEC C14 mains cable (the same type used by desktop PCs).

⚡ Pre-power checklist — complete before first power-on

Go through every item below. Only connect the mains once all boxes are ticked.

  • All three DS18B20 sensors are connected: red to 3.3V, black to GND, yellow to the data bus. The 4.7kΩ pull-up is in place between 3.3V and the data wire.
  • The relay wiring for Heater 1: 5V supply → relay NO, relay COM → Heater 1 wire A, Heater 1 wire B → GND.
  • The MOSFET circuit for Heater 2 is complete with 220Ω gate resistor, 10kΩ pull-down to GND, and heater connected Drain to +5V.
  • The MOSFET circuit for the fan is complete with the 1N5819 flyback diode across the fan terminals (striped end toward +5V).
  • GPIO 17 (pin 11) connects to the Heater 2 MOSFET gate (via 220Ω). GPIO 27 (pin 13) connects to the fan MOSFET gate (via 220Ω).
  • GPIO 4 (pin 7) connects to the DS18B20 data bus. Pin 1 (3.3V) connects to the sensor VCC bus. Pin 9 (GND) connects to the sensor GND bus.
  • The HAT Mini is firmly seated on the stacking header — all 40 pins are engaged.
  • The microSD card is inserted in the Pi.
  • No bare wire ends are touching each other or any metal part of the model house frame.
  • The heaters are mounted on their aluminium plates on a non-flammable surface — not touching foam, cardboard, or raw wood.
  • The 5V supply +V terminal connects to the +5V bus. The −V terminal connects to the GND bus. Polarity is not reversed.

Installing the software

Install Python libraries, confirm 1-Wire is working, and load the test script

Update the Pi's software

Good practice before installing anything new. SSH into the Pi (or type into a directly connected terminal) and run:

Update package list and upgrade installed packages
$ sudo apt update && sudo apt upgrade -y

This may take 3–5 minutes. Wait for it to complete before proceeding.

Install the Automation HAT Mini Python library

Pimoroni provides a one-line installer that handles the library and any system dependencies:

Pimoroni one-line installer (recommended)
$ curl https://get.pimoroni.com/automation | bash

When prompted, say Yes to all options. This takes around 5 minutes and will reboot the Pi at the end. Alternatively, install via pip:

Alternative — install via pip
$ pip3 install automationhat

Verify the 1-Wire sensors are detected

After the reboot, check that the Pi can see the temperature sensors. Load the 1-Wire kernel modules and list detected devices:

Load the modules manually (first time only)
$ sudo modprobe w1-gpio && sudo modprobe w1-therm
List detected 1-Wire devices
$ ls /sys/bus/w1/devices/

You should see three entries beginning with 28- — one per sensor. If you see fewer than three, double-check the wiring for any missing sensors. Each 28-xxxxxxxx directory is a unique sensor ID.

Create the test script

Create a new file on the Pi called test_hardware.py:

Create the test script
$ nano test_hardware.py

Type or paste the following code exactly:

# Hardware test script — Project Dusk model house # Tests: relay, both MOSFETs, and all three temperature sensors import automationhat import RPi.GPIO as GPIO import glob, time GPIO.setmode(GPIO.BCM) GPIO.setup(17, GPIO.OUT) # Heater 2 (MOSFET) GPIO.setup(27, GPIO.OUT) # AC fan (MOSFET) print("--- Testing Relay (Heater 1) ---") automationhat.relay.one.on() print("Relay ON — Heater 1 should activate") time.sleep(3) automationhat.relay.one.off() print("Relay OFF") print("\n--- Testing Heater 2 MOSFET ---") GPIO.output(17, GPIO.HIGH) print("Heater 2 ON") time.sleep(3) GPIO.output(17, GPIO.LOW) print("Heater 2 OFF") print("\n--- Testing Fan MOSFET ---") GPIO.output(27, GPIO.HIGH) print("Fan ON") time.sleep(3) GPIO.output(27, GPIO.LOW) print("Fan OFF") GPIO.cleanup() print("\n--- Reading Temperature Sensors ---") devices = glob.glob('/sys/bus/w1/devices/28*') if not devices: print("ERROR: No sensors found. Check wiring and 1-Wire overlay.") else: for i, d in enumerate(devices): with open(d + '/temperature') as f: temp = int(f.read().strip()) / 1000.0 print(f"Sensor {i+1} (ID: {d.split('/')[-1]}): {temp:.1f} degrees C") print("\nTest complete.")

Press Ctrl+X, then Y, then Enter to save.

Your first test

Power everything on for the first time and run the hardware test

⚡

Confirm the pre-power checklist is complete

Before proceeding, return to Chapter 6 and confirm every item on the pre-power checklist is ticked. This is not optional — it is your last check before live electricity enters the circuit.

First power-on

With everything wired and the pre-power checklist complete, plug the 5V 6A power supply into the mains. Do not touch the wiring while power is connected.

Observe the following over the first 30 seconds:

  • The Raspberry Pi's red power LED should illuminate — it stays steady red.
  • The Pi's green activity LED should flash during boot.
  • The HAT Mini's small display should light up after 20–30 seconds as the Pi boots.
  • No smoke, sparks, or burning smell. If any of these occur: unplug the mains immediately and recheck all wiring.

Allow the Pi 60–90 seconds to complete its boot sequence before trying to SSH in.

SSH in and run the test script

Once booted, SSH into the Pi from your laptop:

On your laptop
$ ssh pi@model-house.local

Then run the test script:

On the Pi
$ python3 test_hardware.py

What success looks like

A fully working system will produce output like this:

--- Testing Relay (Heater 1) --- Relay ON — Heater 1 should activate Relay OFF --- Testing Heater 2 MOSFET --- Heater 2 ON Heater 2 OFF --- Testing Fan MOSFET --- Fan ON Fan OFF --- Reading Temperature Sensors --- Sensor 1 (ID: 28-3ce1041c3fd6): 22.1 degrees C Sensor 2 (ID: 28-3ce1041c4a12): 21.9 degrees C Sensor 3 (ID: 28-3ce1041c5c88): 22.3 degrees C Test complete.

During the test, physically confirm each actuator responds: the heaters should become slightly warm to the touch within 10–15 seconds of activation, and the fan should spin during its 3-second test window.

Note down your sensor IDs

The three 28-xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx IDs are unique to your sensors. Write them down and note which physical location each corresponds to (End 1, Middle, End 2). This allows your simulation software to know which temperature reading comes from where. Label each sensor probe with a small piece of Kapton tape and a permanent marker.

🎉

All tests passing? You're done with the hardware build!

The model house electronics are fully functional. The system is ready for the simulation software integration — the control logic (turning heaters and the fan on and off in response to temperature readings) is handled in the Python control script, where your simulation code connects to the decide() function.

Heater 1 does not warm upRelay not activating, or heater wire looseCheck the relay terminal screws are tight. Listen for a click when the relay activates. Measure voltage across the heater terminals when the relay is ON (should be ~5V).
Heater 2 or Fan does not turn onMOSFET gate not receiving signal, pull-down shorting signalMeasure voltage at the Gate pin when the GPIO is HIGH — should be ~3V. If 0V, check the 220Ω gate resistor. If stuck LOW despite GPIO HIGH, the 10kΩ pull-down may be wired incorrectly (check it goes to GND, not to 5V).
Fan spins briefly at boot before software runsNo gate pull-down resistor installedPower off. Confirm the 10kΩ resistor is wired between the Gate pin and GND. Without it, the gate floats HIGH at startup.
OSError when calling automationhat.relayWrong relay method — calling .relay.on() instead of .relay.one.on()Always use automationhat.relay.one.on() and automationhat.relay.one.off() with the HAT Mini.
Smell of burning or visible smokeShort circuit, wrong polarity, or overloaded wireUnplug mains immediately. Do not reconnect until all wiring has been checked. Look for scorch marks or melted insulation as a clue to the fault location.
